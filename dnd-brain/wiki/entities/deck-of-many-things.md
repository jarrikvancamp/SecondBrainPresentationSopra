# Deck of Many Things

*Wondrous Item — Legendary — No Attunement Required*

See also: [[concepts/magic-items]]

## Overview

The Deck of Many Things is a set of illustrated magical cards — typically 22 in a full deck — each bearing an evocative name. Every card, when drawn, triggers a distinct magical effect. Some effects are enormously beneficial; others are disastrous. The deck is infamous for being one of the most campaign-altering objects in D&D, capable of wiping out a character's wealth in an instant or catapulting them to power.

According to the 2024 DMG, the deck's lore is tied to Euryale and her companion Asteria, whose plea to the gods gave rise to the deck's creation.

## How It Works

1. **Declare draws:** Before drawing, the holder must announce how many cards they intend to draw. Drawing fewer is permissible; drawing more typically results in the excess cards having no effect (or triggers a penalty card).
2. **Draw and resolve:** Each drawn card takes effect immediately. Effects are permanent or long-lasting unless the card's text states otherwise.
3. **Shuffle and reset:** After each draw, the used card magically reappears in the shuffled deck.

## Effect Range

Card effects span a huge spectrum:

- **Beneficial:** Gaining castle deeds or knightly followers, permanent ability score improvements, bonus wishes, piles of gold, or a powerful magic item.
- **Detrimental:** Losing all non-magical wealth and possessions, having a soul imprisoned in a gem somewhere in the outer planes, a cursed enemy marked as a hunter, or losing levels of experience.

The unpredictability is the point — no two draws feel the same, and even a "good" run can be cut short by a catastrophic card.

## Representative Cards (Paraphrased)

**The Star** — One of the deck's more benign cards. Drawing it permanently increases one of the bearer's ability scores, making it a coveted draw for any character.

**The Ruin** — A dreaded card. All non-magical wealth the character possesses — coins, gems, trade goods, property deeds — vanishes instantly and cannot be recovered through ordinary means. Adventurers who drew Ruin after accumulating campaign wealth have had entire economic storylines reset in a single moment.

**The Void** — Perhaps the most feared card. The character's soul is ripped from their body and imprisoned in an object hidden somewhere in the multiverse. The body collapses, inert but alive. Rescuing the trapped soul typically requires a separate quest of significant scope.

## Reputation and Campaign Impact

The Deck of Many Things appears on the Legendary tier of the Arcana random magic item table. In practice, most DMs introduce it as a deliberate plot device rather than a random reward. Groups that encounter the deck often face a compelling choice: the potential for transformation is enormous, but so is the risk of ruin. Drawing from the deck is one of D&D's most famous "are you sure?" moments.

The deck's legend is tied to the card-bearer Euryale, a figure so tied to the deck that her name appears on one of the cards.
