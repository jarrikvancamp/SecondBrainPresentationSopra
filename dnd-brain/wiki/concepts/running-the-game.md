## Overview

The DM's core gameplay loop is a repeating three-step cycle: the DM describes the current situation, the players declare what their characters attempt, and the DM resolves those actions and narrates the outcome. This cycle returns to step one after each resolution.

## The Three-Step Loop

1. **Describe the situation** — communicate what characters can perceive; focus on actionable details and keep descriptions concise (roughly three sentences before inviting questions).
2. **Let the players act** — players state their intentions; outside combat, multiple characters can act simultaneously.
3. **Resolve and narrate** — determine outcomes using the rules or roleplaying judgment, then describe what happens. Some actions need no roll; trivial or impossible tasks should skip the dice entirely.

## Difficulty Class (DC)

Set a DC before asking for a roll. The standard six-tier scale:

| Difficulty       | DC |
|------------------|:--:|
| Very Easy        |  5 |
| Easy             | 10 |
| Moderate         | 15 |
| Hard             | 20 |
| Very Hard        | 25 |
| Nearly Impossible| 30 |

Practical notes:
- **DC 5** — Most characters succeed routinely; skip the roll unless circumstances are unusual.
- **DC 10** — A character with a 10 in the relevant ability and no proficiency succeeds about half the time.
- **DC 15** — Requires either a higher ability score or proficiency for a reasonable chance of success.
- **DC 20** — Typically needs both a solid ability score and proficiency.
- **DC 25–30** — Out of reach for low-level characters; DC 30 requires near-perfect conditions even at level 20.

For DM-set saving throw DCs, stay between 10 and 20. When a creature's own ability drives the effect, use the calculated formula: **DC = 8 + ability modifier + Proficiency Bonus**.

## Calling for Rolls

Only call for a D20 Test when:
- Both success and failure are genuinely possible.
- Failure has meaningful consequences.

Distinguish the roll type:
- **Ability check** — the character is actively trying to accomplish something.
- **Saving throw** — the character is reactively resisting or dodging something.
- **Attack roll** — the character is trying to hit a target.

## Group Checks

When the whole party attempts the same task and skilled members can compensate for weaker ones, call for a group ability check. The group succeeds if at least half the participants succeed individually. Avoid group checks when a single failure would doom the party regardless — use individual rolls instead.

## Advantage and Disadvantage

Roll two d20s and take the higher result (Advantage) or lower result (Disadvantage). Grant Advantage for favorable circumstances, exceptional creative descriptions, or helpful setup actions. Impose Disadvantage when conditions hinder success. Multiple sources of Advantage or Disadvantage do not stack; one of each cancels out entirely.

## Consequences and Degrees

- **Success at a cost** — on a near miss (fail by 1–2), optionally let the player succeed with an attached complication.
- **Degrees of failure** — failing by 5 or more can trigger a worse result than a narrow failure.
- **Critical rolls on checks** — a natural 20 or 1 on an ability check carries no automatic special effect, but the DM may adjust the narrative impact.
