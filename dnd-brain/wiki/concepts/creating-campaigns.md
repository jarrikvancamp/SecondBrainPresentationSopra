A campaign is two or more adventures strung together into a larger story. If an adventure is a TV episode, the campaign is the whole series — self-contained episodes contribute to a bigger arc, while the players' choices shape where it goes.

## Four-Step Process

1. **Lay Out the Premise** — Define the core conflicts and pick a setting that reinforces the tone.
2. **Draw In the Players** — Plan a memorable opening; understand each character's goals.
3. **Plan Adventures** — Design smaller conflicts that feed the larger ones, and flesh out antagonists and key locations.
4. **Bring It to an End** — Decide the target level and how the major conflicts resolve.

## Campaign Journal

A running journal (notebook, binder, wiki, or digital files) tracks sessions, NPCs, locations, and unresolved threads. It enables foreshadowing — planting small details early that pay off later — and keeps an "adventure stockpile" of ideas ready when the party surprises you.

## Premise: Characters, Conflicts, and Setting

### Character Arcs
Characters have **goals** (short-term reasons to adventure), **ambitions** (long-term personal aspirations), and **quirks/whims** that emerge in play. Track motivations and backstory contacts; weave character-focused adventures in occasionally. Give characters significant victories roughly every five levels to invite new goals.

### Campaign Conflicts
Maintain around three active conflicts at different scales. Each should have a conflict arc tied to the tiers of play:

| Tier | Levels | Typical Scope |
|------|:------:|---------------|
| Local Heroes | 1–4 | Village, dungeon, local threat |
| Heroes of the Realm | 5–10 | Region, kingdom |
| Masters of the Realm | 11–16 | Nations, world-shaking events |
| Masters of the World | 17–20 | Planar or cosmic stakes |

When one conflict resolves, replace it with one of broader reach — ideally so that the old conflict turns out to be a piece of the new one.

### Flavors of Fantasy
The campaign's genre shapes tone and conflict types. Common flavors include: heroic fantasy, sword and sorcery, epic fantasy (good vs. evil), mythic fantasy (divine intervention), supernatural horror, war, and science-fantasy mashups. A campaign can mix flavors.

### Campaign Setting
Use a published setting (lore done for you, players may know it) or build your own (complete creative control). Either way, consider:
- Prominent factions and organisations
- How common magic is
- Key mysteries the world holds
- The role of gods

## Session Zero

A pre-campaign session to align expectations before play begins.

### What to Establish
- **Character creation** — recommend varied classes, advise on campaign-appropriate options, set starting level (level 1 for new groups, level 3 for experienced players wanting resilience).
- **Party cohesion** — agree on how characters met: shared history, bonding event, mutual patron, or happenstance.
- **Setting the stage** — share the starting location, key world events, and any house rules.

### Starting Location
Begin with a single well-detailed hub (village, city district, roadside outpost). Add one or two nearby adventure locations. Expand the world outward only as the campaign demands it.

## Plan Adventures: Episodes vs. Serials

- **Episodic** — stand-alone adventures; villains rarely return; good for groups that play infrequently.
- **Serialized** — one continuous story; outcomes of early adventures affect later ones; rewards consistent attendance and creates a sense of momentum.

Linking serials: end one adventure by introducing an object, a fleeing villain, or a clue that leads directly into the next.

## Getting Players Invested

Build recurring elements: a community the characters care about, a home base (see [[concepts/bastions]]), a trusted NPC ally, and a villain with at least one sympathetic quality. Ask players to describe details that matter to their characters — smells, trinkets, childhood memories — then incorporate those into the world.

Insert **break episodes** (low-stakes, off-arc sessions) every three to five levels to give the group space to breathe and develop relationships.

## Ending a Campaign

Wrap up major conflicts and give characters space to complete personal goals. A satisfying ending does not require level 20 — end when the story reaches its natural conclusion. If enthusiasm wanes, discuss with players: they may supply new ideas, a player might take over as DM, or you can arrange a grand finale.

After a campaign ends, its events can become the legends of the next one.
