## Overview

Chapter 3 of the DMG is a reference chapter for situational game elements that don't fit neatly into the basic flow of play. It covers chases, environmental dangers, physical hazards, traps, and poisons — each treated as a modular rule the DM can drop into any session.

## Chases

Standard movement rules make chases predictable (faster creature always wins). The chase system adds randomness:

- All participants roll Initiative and take turns normally.
- Each participant can Dash repeatedly, but after a limit of **3 + Constitution modifier** Dashes they must make a **DC 10 Constitution save** at the end of each turn or gain 1 Exhaustion level. Five Exhaustion levels forces the creature to drop out.
- At the end of each round (Initiative count 0), the quarry makes a **Dexterity (Stealth) check**. If the total beats the lead pursuer's Passive Perception, the quarry escapes. Crowded or obstacle-rich terrain grants Advantage; open terrain gives Disadvantage.
- Each participant rolls 1d12 at the end of their turn and checks the Urban or Wilderness Chase Complications table to see whether an obstacle affects the next creature in Initiative order.

## Environmental Effects

The DMG provides rules for unusual physical conditions, including: extreme cold, extreme heat, frigid water, high altitude, heavy precipitation, slippery ice, strong wind, and special planar zones (wild magic and dead magic zones). Each condition imposes specific mechanical penalties such as reduced Speed, imposed conditions, or mandatory saving throws.

## Hazards

Hazards are static dangers in a location, rated as **nuisance** or **deadly** for a given character-level band. Use caution introducing a hazard to characters one level band below its rating — what nuisances higher-level characters could handle might be lethal to lower-level ones.

Example hazards from the book include:
- **Green Slime** — corrodes nonmagical metal and wood on contact; causes ongoing acid damage to creatures
- **Razorvine** — razor-edged foliage that deals slashing damage to creatures moving through or adjacent to it
- **Brown / Yellow Mold** — environmental fungal dangers that emit cold or spores affecting nearby creatures
- **Poisonous Gas** — gaseous hazards requiring Constitution saves to resist damage or conditions
- **Quicksand Pit, Webs, Rockslide, River Styx** — additional terrain hazards with varying mechanical effects

## Traps

Traps are defined by three elements: **trigger** (the event that activates the trap), **severity** (nuisance or deadly for a level band), and **duration** (instantaneous, timed, or until destroyed/dispelled).

**Common triggers:** pressure plate, trip wire, doorknob, wrong key, proximity.

**Damage scaling** (for custom trap design):

| Levels | Nuisance Damage | Deadly Damage |
|:------:|:---------------:|:-------------:|
| 1–4    | ~1d10           | ~2d10         |
| 5–10   | ~2d10           | ~4d10         |
| 11–16  | ~4d10           | ~10d10        |
| 17–20  | ~10d10          | ~18d10        |

Attack bonuses for traps are roughly +4 (nuisance) or +8 (deadly) across all levels; save DCs scale from roughly 10–12 at low levels to 19–21 at the highest tier.

The book advises using traps sparingly to preserve their surprise value. The best traps are either brief obstacles skilled characters can bypass quickly, or puzzles that reward teamwork.

## Poisons

Poisons are grouped by delivery method:

| Type     | Delivery mechanism |
|----------|--------------------|
| **Contact**  | Smeared on an object; activates when a creature touches it with bare skin |
| **Ingested** | Must be swallowed as a full dose (in food or drink) |
| **Inhaled**  | Released as a powder or gas in a 5-foot cube; holding one's breath does not prevent exposure |
| **Injury**   | Applied to a weapon or ammunition; activates when the coated object deals piercing or slashing damage |

Harvesting poison from a defeated or incapacitated venomous creature takes 1d6 minutes and requires a **DC 20 Intelligence (Nature) check** with a Poisoner's Kit. Success yields one dose; failing by 5 or more exposes the harvester to the creature's poison.

## Random Tables

Chapter 3 provides several random-generation tables (chase complications, monster personality, initial attitudes, settlement traits, and more) to help DMs quickly populate or vary encounters without extensive preparation. These are tools for improvisation, not prescriptive rules.
