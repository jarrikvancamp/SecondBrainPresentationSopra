An adventure is a self-contained story built from scenes of combat, exploration, and social interaction. It is not a fixed narrative — the players' choices shape how it unfolds.

## Four-Step Process

1. **Lay Out the Premise** — Establish the core situation or conflict, its setting, and what makes it interesting.
2. **Draw In the Players** — Decide how the characters get pulled into the situation; tie hooks to their goals.
3. **Plan Encounters** — Map out the scenes that take the party from start to finish.
4. **Bring It to an End** — Consider likely endings and what rewards await.

## Premise and Conflict

A premise is a situation worth investigating — a brewing war, a monstrous migration, a rumored dungeon. It needs a **conflict** to drive it: a villain, rival factions, a natural disaster, or even tension within the party. The four tiers of play suggest appropriate scope:

| Levels | Scale |
|--------|-------|
| 1–4 | Local (village, dungeon) |
| 5–10 | Regional (realm, kingdom) |
| 11–16 | National or world-shaking |
| 17–20 | Planar or cosmic |

See also: [[creating-campaigns]] for how tiers connect across a campaign.

## Adventure Hooks

A hook draws characters into the premise. Common categories:

- **Patron Hook** — An NPC commissions the party directly, through a job board, or by letter.
- **Supernatural Hook** — A divine vision, prophetic dream, or omen points toward the adventure.
- **Happenstance Hook** — The situation finds the party by coincidence: a letter discovered, a festival disrupted, a chance encounter.

## Encounters: Building Blocks

Each encounter has an objective and an obstacle. Encounters accomplish one or more of: advancing characters toward a goal, frustrating progress, or revealing information.

### Encounter Types
- **Combat** — Fights with monsters or NPCs; vary elevation, terrain hazards, and mixed enemy groups to add tactical interest.
- **Exploration** — Traps, secret doors, environmental dangers, and travel through hazardous terrain.
- **Social Interaction** — Negotiation, persuasion, or deception with NPCs.

### Sample Objectives
Protect an NPC, retrieve an object, stop a ritual, sneak past guards, run a gauntlet, make peace between factions, take out a single target.

## Encounter Pacing and Tension

Tension builds naturally as characters spend limited resources. To keep pacing dynamic:

- **Vary encounter type** — alternate combat, exploration, and social scenes.
- **Vary difficulty** — mix low, moderate, and high-difficulty encounters, reserving the hardest for the climax.
- **Vary threats** — use different monster types, hazards, and NPC personalities.

**Rests and urgency** are in natural tension: resting eases resource pressure but may cost the party time. Scatter safe-rest opportunities deliberately, and occasionally interrupt rests to maintain stakes.

**Random encounters** can drain resources, create urgency, establish atmosphere, or provide unexpected story moments.

### Combat Difficulty Budget

Difficulty is calibrated by XP budget per character (multiply by party size):

| Level | Low | Moderate | High |
|:-----:|----:|:--------:|-----:|
| 1 | 50 | 75 | 100 |
| 5 | 500 | 750 | 1,100 |
| 10 | 1,600 | 2,300 | 3,100 |
| 20 | 10,000 | 15,000 | 22,000 |

## Structure: Linear vs. Open

- A **linear** structure moves the party through scenes in sequence — clear but risks feeling like a railroad.
- An **open / point-crawl** structure offers multiple threads or locations at once, giving the party meaningful choices; unresolved threads can escalate into bigger problems.

To avoid railroading, plan multiple ways for key events to come about, and account for the possibility that players prevent those events entirely.

## Adventure Endings

A climax should feel earned — telegraphed by earlier foreshadowing. After the climax, a **denouement** ties up loose threads: distributing treasure, roleplaying the aftermath, and seeding the next adventure.

Common climax shapes: final boss battle, villain pursuit, race against time, multiple simultaneous rites, betrayal, or an escaping villain.

## Rewards

Adventures yield treasure (individual loot and hoards by CR) and possibly quest rewards. Non-material rewards — reputation, titles, contacts — matter too. See [[creating-campaigns]] for how rewards feed long-term play.
